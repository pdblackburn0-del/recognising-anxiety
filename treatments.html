Running (Aerobic Exercise)

Running reduces anxiety by activating the body’s natural stress-relief systems. Aerobic exercise lowers cortisol, increases circulation to the brain, and boosts endorphins and endocannabinoids—chemicals associated with calm and emotional regulation. It also interrupts rumination by engaging attention in rhythmic movement and breathing, while long-term training strengthens stress resilience and improves sleep, both of which directly reduce anxiety.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

CBT is one of the most effective treatments for anxiety because it helps people identify unhelpful thinking patterns and replace them with more realistic, balanced thoughts. By challenging catastrophic predictions and gradually modifying avoidance behaviors, CBT retrains the brain’s response to perceived threats. Over time, this reduces fear responses and builds confidence in coping skills, making anxiety far less disruptive.

Mindfulness Meditation

Mindfulness works by teaching the brain to observe thoughts and sensations without reacting to them. This reduces the automatic “fight-or-flight” response that fuels anxiety. Regular practice strengthens neural pathways involved in emotional regulation and decreases activity in the brain’s fear centers. As a result, people become better at staying grounded in the present moment instead of spiraling into anxious what-ifs.

SSRI Medication (Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors)

SSRIs help treat anxiety by increasing serotonin availability in the brain, which improves mood stability and reduces the intensity of fear signals. Over several weeks, SSRIs regulate communication between brain regions involved in threat detection and emotional processing, leading to fewer physical symptoms like racing heart or tension. For many, medication creates enough stability to fully benefit from therapy or lifestyle changes

Exposure Therapy

Exposure therapy reduces anxiety by helping the brain unlearn the association between feared situations and danger. Gradually and repeatedly confronting the trigger teaches the nervous system that the situation is safe, reducing avoidance behaviors. Over time, the fear response weakens through a process known as extinction learning, allowing people to regain normal functioning and confidence in situations they once avoided.

Deep Breathing / Diaphragmatic Breathing

Deep breathing activates the parasympathetic nervous system—the body’s relaxation mode—which counteracts the elevated heart rate, muscle tension, and rapid breathing associated with anxiety. By slowing the breath and engaging the diaphragm, oxygen levels stabilize and the brain receives signals that the threat has passed. This physiological shift helps calm both the body and mind, making anxiety far easier to manage in the moment.
